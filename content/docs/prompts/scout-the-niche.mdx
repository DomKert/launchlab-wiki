## Why this works

Perplexity is faster than Claude for live web research and cites sources. You want a map of who's already serving your customer, not a wall of text. 300 words in, 2 gaps out.

## Prompt

```txt
I'm launching [ONE-LINE DESCRIPTION]. My customer is [CUSTOMER SENTENCE from Prompt 1].

Find 5 to 8 real, currently-active competitors or substitutes. For each:
- Name and one-line positioning
- Who they target specifically
- Where they're weak (pricing, audience mismatch, dated brand, poor distribution, whatever you see)

End with: the 1 or 2 gaps in this market I could actually win in as a first-time builder this weekend. Keep it under 300 words total. Cite sources inline.
```

## Back Room sample answer (truncated)

> **The RealReal.** Luxury consignment, millions of SKUs, mass-market. Weak. Not curated, dated brand, aggressive email discounting trains bad habits.
>
> **Vestiaire Collective.** Peer-to-peer luxury resale, Europe-leaning. Weak. Buyer does the hunting, quality unpredictable.
>
> **1stdibs.** High-end antique and vintage dealer marketplace. Weak. Priced for interior designers, not personal wardrobe.
>
> **Depop.** Gen Z streetwear vintage. Weak. Wrong demo for 35 to 59 creative women.
>
> **Small curated Substack vintage sellers.** Niche taste authorities, 200 to 2,000 subscribers. Weak. No gated access, no live-drop urgency, open to anyone.
>
> **The gap.** Invite-gated + taste-authority-curated + live-drop format doesn't exist for this demo. Substack curators get the taste right but skip the gate and the event. Marketplaces get the scale but not the curation. The Back Room sits in the middle. Gated like a private club, curated like a Substack, staged like a drop.
